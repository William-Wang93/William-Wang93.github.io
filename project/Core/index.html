A Visualization of Core Income

January 15, 2022 * William Wang * Visualize Financial Statements

When you work long enough in a high growth company, you tend to hear a lot about the concept of "Core Income", which measures the earnings generated from the principal business lines for the company. The reason this concept holds weight at young, fast growing companies is that investors are often keen on understanding the true value of these newer, less understood businesses. And to do that, they need to understand not only how much money the company makes over a year, but how much of those earnings are sustainable and principal to the company over the long term.

In other words, what's real, and what's noise? What happened this year that's likely to happen next year?

I'll give an example. A company might change its inventory valuation method from FIFO to LIFO, and retroactively changes it Cost of Goods Sold in previous years to reflect this change.

In the current year, the company would make an adjusting entry to recognize the gain or loss for the change in the method, which would alter Net Income and its cascading domino tiles we call financial ratios that affect how financiers view investments.

But this wouldn't be viewed as Core Income for the company. Unless the company was in the business of annually adjusting its accounting policies (which, by the way, would only only infuriate its entire accounting team), the adjustment would be supplementary income.

With some HTML and CSS, I"ve created a visualization that walks down Core Income to Supplementary Income, all the way down to Comprehensive Income. Clicking on each box will give an explanation of each line item.

Core Income:

This is the revenue from the main lines of a business. If an apple cider making company listed out it's core income on its income statement, it would be cider making (and ancillary sales of apples). It probably wouldn't list its gains on investments of NFTs, however.

This is the cost of Sales that is incurred to manufacture core goods/deliver services. Back to the cider making company example - You need apples to make cider, your core revenue, so that would be a COGS.
